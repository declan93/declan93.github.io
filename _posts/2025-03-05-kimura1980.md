**Reference:** Kimura M. A simple method for estimating evolutionary rates of base substitutions through comparative studies of nucleotide sequences. *J Mol Evol.* 1980;16(2):111–120.

---

## The Problem

When you compare two homologous DNA sequences, the fraction of sites that differ — the raw *p*-distance — is a biased underestimate of the true number of substitutions that have occurred. Sites can be hit more than once (superimposed changes), and changes can revert. Any serious estimate of evolutionary distance must correct for this.

Jukes and Cantor (1969) provided the first such correction, assuming all four nucleotides substitute into one another at equal rates. This gives:

$$K = -\frac{3}{4} \log_e \left(1 - \frac{4}{3}\lambda\right)$$

where λ = P + Q is the fraction of differing sites. The problem: in real sequences — especially at third codon positions — **transitions** (purine↔purine or pyrimidine↔pyrimidine: A↔G, C↔T) occur substantially more often than **transversions** (purine↔pyrimidine: A/G↔C/T). Jukes-Cantor ignores this asymmetry and can therefore produce poor distance estimates when the transition/transversion ratio is high.

---

## The Key Idea: Separate the Two Substitution Types

Kimura defines two categories of difference when comparing two homologous sequences of *n* sites:

- **Type I (transitions):** both bases are purines, or both are pyrimidines (e.g. A↔G, C↔T). Frequency: *P* = n₁/n
- **Type II (transversions):** one base is a purine, the other a pyrimidine (e.g. A↔C, A↔T, G↔C, G↔T). Frequency: *Q* = n₂/n

He then models evolution with two rate parameters (Fig. 1 in the paper):

- **α** = rate of transitions per site per year
- **β** = rate of transversions per site per year (two possible transversion paths per site, each at rate β, so total transversion rate = 2β)

Total substitution rate: **k = α + 2β**

This is the "two-parameter model", now universally called **K2P** or **K80**.

---

## The Derivation

Kimura writes differential equations for how *P(T)* and *Q(T)* evolve over time, tracking all 16 possible base-pair combinations at homologous sites across two lineages that diverged *T* years ago. The solutions are:

$$P(T) = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{2}e^{-4(\alpha+\beta)T} + \frac{1}{4}e^{-8\beta T}$$

$$Q(T) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2}e^{-8\beta T}$$

Inverting these to express evolutionary time in terms of observable proportions P and Q:

$$4(\alpha + \beta)T = -\log_e(1 - 2P - Q)$$

$$8\beta T = -\log_e(1 - 2Q)$$

Since the total number of substitutions separating the two sequences is K = 2kT = 2(α + 2β)T (two branches each of length T), combining the above gives the central result:

$$\boxed{K = -\frac{1}{2} \log_e \left\{(1 - 2P - Q)\sqrt{1 - 2Q}\right\}}$$

This is the **Kimura two-parameter distance**. When α = β (equal rates — the Jukes-Cantor scenario), it reduces exactly to the Jukes-Cantor formula.

---

## The Synonymous Rate Extension

Kimura also derives the synonymous component of the substitution rate. At third codon positions, roughly half the sites are fourfold degenerate (all changes synonymous) and half are twofold degenerate (transitions synonymous, transversions non-synonymous). The synonymous substitution rate per site is therefore approximately k_S = α + β, giving:

$$K_S = -\frac{1}{2} \log_e(1 - 2P - Q)$$

Applied to globin sequences, Kimura showed that synonymous substitutions occur at rates roughly 5× higher than amino acid-altering ones — an early quantitative demonstration of purifying selection constraining protein-coding sequences.

---

## Why It Matters

**Practical:** K2P is still the default distance model in many phylogenetic tools (e.g., MEGA) and is the basis of the standard formula used to estimate synonymous (*K*S) and non-synonymous (*K*A) substitution rates — the *K*A/*K*S ratio that is foundational to tests of selection in comparative genomics.

**Conceptual:** The paper is a clean example of how to build a continuous-time Markov model on a finite state space (4 nucleotides), solve the resulting ODEs, and invert the solution to get a statistically useful estimator. The same framework — with more parameters — underlies all modern substitution models (HKY85, GTR, etc.).

**Limitation acknowledged by Kimura:** The model still assumes equal base composition and homogeneous rates across sites. When the transition/transversion ratio is very high (>10), or rate heterogeneity is substantial, more parameter-rich models perform better. But for moderate divergence and moderate ts/tv bias, K2P is remarkably robust.

## The Formula at a Glance

| Quantity | Formula |
|---|---|
| Evolutionary distance (all subs) | K = −½ ln{(1−2P−Q)√(1−2Q)} |
| Synonymous distance | K_S = −½ ln(1−2P−Q) |
| Rate per year | k = K / (2T) |

Where P = fraction of transitional differences, Q = fraction of transversional differences.
